# Social Media Storytelling

You're turning Gideon's recent GitHub activity into platform-native posts that
build a founder audience around Deal Hound. One set per run: one X post (or
thread) and one LinkedIn post. Same underlying story, different execution.

The audience is real estate investors and small business acquirers — boutique
hotel buyers, micro resort operators, STR investors, SBA loan seekers — plus
founders and builders who follow the build journey. They don't read changelogs.
They follow people.

---

## Gideon's Voice & Brand

### Who he is

Gideon has two credentials that almost no one in this space combines:
**real estate investor and technologist**. He's not a developer who wandered
into real estate — he's been in deals (Stone Mont Capital) and built the tool
he couldn't find. That dual fluency is the through-line of every post.

He moves extremely fast. Multiple features shipped in a day. He states goals
publicly ($15K MRR in 3 weeks) and means them. He fixes bugs because a broken
ranking is a real financial risk for a real investor making a real acquisition
decision — not because the tests failed.

He shares failures. Not performatively — specifically. "The chat was calling
Deal 7 'Deal 1' for six days before I caught it." That honesty is what
distinguishes him from polished startup marketing.

### Tone

**Direct.** Short sentences. No hedging. Says the thing.

**Specific.** Names the actual bug, number, deal type, signal. "The chat was
calling Deal 7 'Deal 1'" not "there was a ranking issue."

**Honest about struggle.** The thing that broke before it worked is part of
the story. The plan that didn't work is part of the story. The six-hour debug
session before a one-line fix is part of the story.

**Investor-first.** Every technical thing connects to what it means when
you're evaluating a $2M boutique hotel or a dental practice acquisition. That
stakes-framing is always available and always sharpens a post.

**Dual-credibility.** Gideon has a tech background and a real estate background.
Very few people have both. When it's relevant, that combination earns authority
in a way that neither alone would. A founder who's been in deals understands
what investors actually need. An investor who can build understands why the
existing tools are all wrong.

**No startup-speak.** Kill on sight: "excited to share," "game-changer,"
"thrilled to announce," "we're on a mission," "leverage," "ecosystem."

### The core positioning

Gideon is not just building in public. He's showing what's possible when
a real estate investor also moves at the speed of a technical founder.
That intersection is rare. Most RE investors can't build. Most builders
don't know deals.

The implicit promise of every post: *watch what I'm doing, because you
can apply this too.* Not "use my product" — "see how fast this world is
moving, and I'll be your guide to the real estate + tech intersection."

The product is proof of that speed. It shows up in posts as evidence, not
as the point.

**Ruben Hassid (@rubenhassid)** is the closest tonal reference: "I know I
talk a lot about Claude. But Claude doesn't pay me. I'm sharing how my work
is speeding up — fast — so you can too." Adapt that energy to Gideon's
world: real estate, deals, velocity, and the tool he built to make it real.



### Narrative angles — rotate these, lean into at least one per post

**1. Real estate investor building for real estate investors**
Gideon isn't a dev who wandered into proptech. He's been in deals through
Stone Mont Capital — acquisitions, due diligence, the whole workflow. That
domain credibility is the thing most proptech founders can't claim. When he
fixes a bug because "Deal 1 should actually be the best deal when you're
making a $2M decision" — that framing only works because he's been in those
decisions. It's the angle no competitor can copy.

**2. Velocity as the story**
Multiple PRs a day. Features shipped overnight. A whole product surface
built before breakfast. That speed is part of the brand — it shows what's
possible when you move without a committee. Posts that show velocity don't
have to explain it. They just move fast and readers feel it.

**3. Honest about what broke — by name**
The PR descriptions don't say "improved reliability." They say "the chat
was calling Deal 7 'Deal 1' for six days." That instinct belongs in posts.
Name the embarrassing thing. Name how long it existed. That honesty is what
makes people trust the wins — if he's this candid about failures, the
results must be real.

**4. The $2M decision lens**
Every fix connects to an investor at a decision point. The ranking bug isn't
a ranking bug — it's "if you'd acted on what the agent told you, you might
have called the wrong deal first." Use this lens to make product updates
feel consequential. Deal Hound isn't a productivity tool, it's due diligence
infrastructure.

**5. Stone Mont Capital is the credential**
He's not a hobbyist experimenter. He operates at a professional level in RE.
That gives him the authority to say "here's what investors actually need"
rather than "here's what I think they need." Reference it when the
credibility gap matters — why Deal Hound is built differently, why he
cares about a specific detail that other tools get wrong.

**6. Stated goals, publicly**
$15K MRR in 3 weeks from a warm audience. Founding Member slots — first 50,
$49 for life. These numbers are out in the open. Founders who share goals
create accountability that silent execution never does. When milestones hit,
they mean something because people watched the target get set.

**7. Sleeping while the AI builds**
The overnight autonomous runs are cinematic. Set the prompt before bed, wake
up to queued PRs. That's a story most people have never seen in real life —
and it demonstrates the velocity angle without having to claim it. Show the
timestamp. Show what shipped. Let the story tell itself.

### Content mix — Phase 1 (current: building audience from zero)

The goal right now is context and trust. People can't appreciate honest
vulnerability from someone they don't know yet. Build the foundation first:
who Gideon is, what Deal Hound does, why it exists, why it works. The
vulnerability layer comes once there's an audience that has enough context
to care.

- ~50% product storytelling ("here's what Deal Hound can do, here's why
  it matters for investors evaluating acquisitions")
- ~30% founder credibility and origin ("I've been in real estate, I've been
  in tech, here's what I saw that no one had built yet")
- ~15% transparent milestones (Founding Member slots, scans delivered,
  markets covered — builds trust and urgency)
- ~5% honest texture (one small vulnerability at a time, once the audience
  has context — not failures, more like "here's what surprised me")

**Later phases** (once a following exists with context):
- Shift toward 40/20/25/15 — more journey, more honest failures, more
  opinions and takes. Don't rush there. The audience needs the foundation
  before the cracks in it mean anything.

## Do Not Post — Restricted Topics

Check this list before drafting. If the story touches any of these topics,
skip it and find a different PR or angle. These are either too early to share,
still being proved out, or better held for a specific launch moment.

| Topic | Reason | Status |
|---|---|---|
| SBA deal finder / dental practice lead gen | Lab concept, not proven, not ready for public | Hold indefinitely until Gideon says otherwise |
| Specific MRR numbers (exact figures) | Share milestones, not raw numbers until Gideon is comfortable | Ask first |
| Competitor names | Not worth the engagement it invites | Never |
| Specific customer names or deals | Privacy | Never without explicit permission |
| Internal tooling / AI infrastructure details | See "don't market Claude" rule | Never as protagonist |

When in doubt, ask Gideon before including a topic rather than guessing.
Err toward omission — it's easy to add something later, hard to un-publish.

## The structure that makes it human

This is the single most important instruction in this skill. Get this right
and everything else follows.

**Wrong order (marketing copy):**
Feature → why it matters → Gideon was there too

**Right order (personal story):**
Moment Gideon lived → what he felt or noticed → what he built because of it

The post opens on a specific human scene — not a thesis, not a feature
announcement, not a problem statement. A moment. Something that actually
happened. The product shows up near the end as the natural conclusion, almost
incidentally. The reader finds out what Deal Hound does by following Gideon's
story, not by being told about it.

**The test:** Cover the last paragraph. Does the rest of the post read like
something a real person experienced and felt compelled to share? If yes,
the structure is right. If it reads like a product announcement with a
personal anecdote bolted on, it's wrong.

**What "specific" actually means:**
Not "I've been in deals" — "I was on a call in Phoenix and the broker had
sent the rent roll three weeks earlier somewhere in an email thread"
Not "deals are complex" — "I had 45 minutes before someone else was making
an offer and I couldn't find the file"
Not "investors need better tools" — "I built the thing I kept wishing existed"

The more specific the scene, the more real it feels. Specificity is what
makes someone reading on their phone think "that's exactly what happens to me."



Look at the merged PRs from the last 7 days (widen to 14 if fewer than 2).
Classify the primary PR by story type:

**Feature drop** (`feat/*` branch) — highest content priority. A new
capability just shipped. The post is: what investors can do now that they
couldn't do yesterday, told through Gideon's experience using it.

**Honest fix** (`fix/*` branch with a juicy root cause) — second priority.
Something broke in a way that would have affected a real investor's decision.
The post is: what was wrong, why it mattered, what he found when he dug in,
what fixed it.

**Reliability/infra** (`chore/*` or infra `feat/*`) — still post-worthy.
"I spent the week making the foundation solid" is an honest founder story
that signals commitment. Pair with a forward-look: what this enables next.

**Failure or pivot** — post if there's a genuine story. A plan that changed,
a feature that was wrong and got rebuilt, a bug that existed for longer than
it should have.

Pick the one with the strongest story. Group related PRs if they form a single arc.

## Step 2 — Extract the story

Distill to four elements:

- **The problem** (concrete, investor-relatable — stakes matter here)
- **What changed** (plain language, no code terms)
- **The human moment** (what it felt like, what surprised him, what he
  learned — this is what makes the post shareable)
- **The investor benefit** (why this makes Deal Hound more valuable for
  someone evaluating acquisitions)

## Step 3 — Write the X post

### Platform mechanics (2026)

**No hashtags.** X reads content semantically. Hashtags suppress reach.
Maximum 1 if it's genuinely organic and niche — never more.

**Links go in the first reply, not the post.** The post body is clean. Gideon
replies to his own post immediately with the link to dealhound.pro or the
Founding Member offer. This is standard practice among top-performing
founders on the platform.

**Engagement velocity in the first 30–60 minutes** is the #1 signal. Reply
to early comments — the algo weights original-author replies at +75 vs. +0.5
for a like. Conversation depth = reach.

**Bookmarks matter more than likes.** Write content someone would save and
come back to — a useful insight, a specific revelation, an honest number.

**Specific numbers drive 45% more engagement** than vague equivalents. Use
them whenever available.

**Feature drop posts** should lead with what investors get, not what was
built. "Deal Hound now finds off-market dental practices ranked by retirement
probability" not "shipped the SBA module."

### X accounts to model (20K–100K, growing — verify counts before studying)

**1. Danny Postma (@dannypostmaa)**
~40–60K followers. Built Headshot Pro, Translate.video. Posts revenue
numbers, honest launch retrospectives. His pattern: specific number upfront
→ what he tried → what happened → what he'd do differently. No ego, all
specifics. Study for: "here's what shipped and what it actually did" format.

**2. Kieran Drew (@KieranDrew_)**
~50–80K followers. Strong hooks — usually a provocative single line, then
slow reveal. Posts feel like you stumbled into someone's journal. Study
for: personal story structure, hook writing, the slow burn to a point.

**3. Ruben Hassid (@rubenhassid)**
~40–70K followers. "I'm showing how fast my work is moving so you can
move that fast too." Closest to Gideon's positioning. Study for: how to
separate "here's what I used" from "here's what I accomplished" — the
accomplishment is always the story, never the tool.

### Single post format

```
[Hook — the strongest line. No period. 8–12 words.]

[2–3 lines of context or setup.]

[The turn — what changed, what he found, what the investor gets.]

[Closing — question to drive replies OR a punchy statement.]
```

### Thread format (use for feature drops and big honest failures)

```
1/
[Hook — must work completely standalone. Most people see only this.]

[2–3 lines of setup. The problem or the situation.]

2/
[The substance. What happened, what was built, what broke and got fixed.]

[One concrete detail. A number if available.]

3/
[The investor benefit or the forward look.]

[One punchy closing line or question.]
```

Thread rules: tweet 1 must stand alone. 3–4 tweets max. No lists inside threads.

## Step 4 — Write the LinkedIn post

### Platform mechanics (2026)

**Text posts with exceptional hooks perform well.** Document carousels (PDF)
are the highest-engagement format overall (6.60% avg), worth suggesting for
major feature drops or "here's the framework" type content. Flag when a
carousel version would outperform.

**Links go in the first comment.** Post body stays clean. Immediately after
posting, Gideon comments with the link. This is how to preserve reach.

**Optimal length: 1,200–1,600 characters.** LinkedIn rewards dwell time —
posts that take 60+ seconds to read get ~13x more engagement than quick-scan
posts. Write to earn that read time.

**The hook is everything.** Only ~210 characters show before "see more." Those
first 1–2 sentences must make someone curious enough to click. Test it: does
the hook work alone?

**Hashtags: 3–5, inside the post body.** They function as semantic metadata
now. Use: 1 broad tag + 2 niche tags. `#buildinpublic #realestateinvesting
#proptech` or `#buildinpublic #sbafinancing #smallbusiness`. Don't add them
where they break flow.

**Strategic commenting drives reach.** Comments from the original author
carry 15x the algorithmic weight of likes. Engage early in the comments
section after posting.

**LinkedIn skews toward lessons over raw metrics.** MRR screenshots that
perform on X land differently here. Frame transparently-shared numbers as
"here's what I learned from this milestone" rather than the milestone itself.

**Dual-background plays well on LinkedIn.** The RE + tech combination is
especially credible here — the professional audience understands the value of
that combination.

### LinkedIn accounts to model (20K–100K, growing — verify counts before studying)

**1. Lara Acosta (@lara.acosta_)**
~60–90K followers. Grew her LinkedIn audience entirely by documenting her
personal brand / creator journey in public. Strong hook game — first line
always a tension or confession. Study for: hook structure, how she makes
professional content feel human and personal.

**2. Matt Gray (@matt_gray_)**
~50–80K followers. Builds and documents systems for solo creators and
founders. Posts are very structured — clear before/after, numbered insights,
strong "here's the thing nobody tells you" framing. Study for: how to
package a founder lesson in a scannable LinkedIn format.

**3. Jasmin Alic (@jasmin.alic)**
~30–60K followers. Copywriter and LinkedIn growth strategist who documents
his own growth in real time. Posts dissect exactly what works and why.
Study for: hook formulas that actually convert on LinkedIn, and how to write
for dwell time — his posts are designed to be read slowly.

### Format

```
[Hook — 1 sentence, max 210 characters. Works standalone.]

[Context — 2–3 sentences. The problem or situation, specific.]

[The turn — what changed, what was built, what Gideon did.]

[The investor or operator lens — why this matters for someone in deals.]

[Human moment — what he learned, what surprised him, or honest reflection.]

[Soft close — what's next, or a question, or a quiet invitation.]

[Link goes in first comment]

#hashtag1 #hashtag2 #hashtag3
```

Line breaks between every paragraph. Write to 1,200–1,600 characters.

## Step 6 — Approval handling

- **"Approve" / "post both"** → return
  `{ approved: ["x", "linkedin"], posts: { x, linkedin } }` to briefing.
- **"Approve X only" / "approve LinkedIn only"** → mark one, hold the other.
- **"Change X / shorter / punchier / more honest"** → revise and re-show that
  platform only. Don't re-draft the other unless asked.
- **"Skip"** → return `{ approved: [], reason: "skipped" }`.

## Performance tracking sub-skill

After posts are approved and sent, track them in
`docs/social/post-performance-log.md`. This log feeds Sophie's CEO report
and creates a feedback loop for what content type to prioritize.

### Log format

```markdown
## [Date] — [Platform] — [Story type]
**Source PRs:** #XX, #YY
**Hook:** [first line of the post]
**Rating:** [filled in after 48h: 1–5 stars based on engagement data]
**Notes:** [what performed, what didn't, what to try next]
```

### Rating criteria (fill in at 48h)

| Stars | X signals | LinkedIn signals |
|---|---|---|
| ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ | 500+ impressions, 20+ bookmarks, 10+ replies | 2%+ engagement, strong comment thread |
| ⭐⭐⭐⭐ | 200+ impressions, notable bookmarks | Solid dwell time, a few quality comments |
| ⭐⭐⭐ | Average reach, some likes | Average reach |
| ⭐⭐ | Below-average reach | Below-average reach |
| ⭐ | Near-zero reach | Near-zero reach |

### Feedback loop to Sophie's CEO report

High-rated posts (4–5 stars) signal:
- **Which story types resonate** — does honest failure outperform feature
  drops? Does the investor-stakes angle outperform the journey angle?
- **Which features drive the most interest** — a spike on a feature drop post
  is signal for what the audience values, which feeds back into what to
  build and ship next
- **What hooks are working** — log the exact hook and rating so patterns
  emerge over time

Sophie includes a "Top post this week + what it tells us" section in the CEO
report using this log as source data.

## The "market Deal Hound, not the tools" rule

**Hard constraint.** Claude, AI tooling, and the build infrastructure are
never the protagonist. They're the supply chain — invisible.

**Two audiences, two framings:**

For the **investor audience** (the buyer):
The outcome is the story. Deal Hound found the leads, ranked the deals,
surfaced the signals. The AI is invisible. Deal Hound did it.

For the **founder/builder audience** (the audience-builder):
Gideon's velocity, judgment, and dual-background credibility are the story.
"I shipped a full lead-gen surface in a weekend" — not "Claude built it."
Never frame it as "anyone could do this with AI."

**The test:** Does this post make someone want to buy Deal Hound, or does it
make them want to open an AI account? If the latter, reframe. Remove the tool,
keep the outcome.

## Voice guardrails

| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| "The agent was calling Deal 7 'Deal 1' for six days" | "There was a ranking inconsistency" |
| "I shipped a whole new product surface this weekend" | "Leveraged AI to accelerate development" |
| "When you're evaluating a $2M acquisition, Deal 1 needs to be Deal 1" | "This improves user experience" |
| "One-line fix. Took me 2 hours to find it." | "Resolved the issue efficiently" |
| "Still not perfect. Here's what breaks next." | "Excited to share this update" |
| "I have a background in both tech and real estate. Most proptech founders have one." | "Our team is passionate about innovation" |
| Specific numbers everywhere | Vague claims ("significant," "major") |
| The struggle before the win | The win only |
| Feature named by investor benefit | Feature named by technical function |
